
INTRODUCTION
The matter exists in three fundamental states, the gas, liquid and solid. Besides these, another state is Plasma state (a partially ionised gas at very high temperature). Gases are the simplest state of matter to study. This is because the gaseous particles have great freedom of motion and move randomly in a straight line until they collide with each other or with the walls of the container. Therefore, they possess kinetic energy and occupy all the space available to them. The rates of diffusion are different for different gases. When diffusion takes place between a liquid and a gas, it is known as evaporation. The earth's atmosphere is a mixture of gases. Oxygen (21%) and Nitrogen (78%) are its major components, both support life. In fact, the earth's atmosphere is an immense, system in which many chemical reactions occur. For example, photosynthesis, combustion etc. Natural gas, the major component of which is methane, is used to heat homes in winter, and to drive vehicles etc.

KINETIC MOLECULAR THEORY OF GASES
The behaviour and properties of gases can be theoretically explained using the kinetic molecular theory. This theory is a model that explains the behaviour of gases using idealization about random moving molecules within a gas. The kinetic molecular theory was first postulated by Daniel Bernoulli, a Swiss mathematician. The Kinetic theory of gases was elaborated and extended by a number of well-known physicist such as James Maxwell (1859) and Boltzmann in (1870). In 1857, Clausius derived the kinetic equation and deduced all the gas laws from it. Physical theories are often given in terms of postulates, the basic statements from which all conclusions or prediction of theory is deduced.

Kinetic Molecular Theory
The kinetic molecular theory of gases, is based upon the following postulates: Gases are considered to be composed of minute discrete particles called molecules. The molecules move randomly in straight lines until they collide with one another or with the walls of container. The collisions among the molecules are perfectly elastic i.e. the total kinetic energy remains constant. The molecules of a gas are thought to be of the same mass and size but are different from gas to gas. The pressure is produced due to the collisions of the molecules. The average kinetic energy of a molecule is directly proportional to the absolute temperature. In other words, the higher the temperature, greater will be the molecular kinetic energy. At relatively low pressure, the average distances between molecules are large compared with molecular diameters. The attractive forces between the molecules are negligible. The repulsive forces between the molecules are negligible. The volume of the molecules is negligible as compared to the volume of the container.

The root mean square velocity of gas molecules: All molecules do not have the same velocity at a given temperature and pressure. It is the average velocity which is considered. If n1 molecules have velocity C1, n2 Molecules have velocity C2, and so on, then the mean square velocity (c^2) is given by: c^2 = (n1c1^2 + n2c2^2 + ...) / (n1 + n2 + ...). It is the mean average of squares of all the possible velocities. The square root of c^2 is called root mean square velocity (Crms).

Motion of Particles of a Gas
According to the kinetic molecular theory, the gaseous molecules are always in a state of continuous motion. As a result due to their motion, they will have certain kinetic energy. The increase or decrease of temperature will increase or decrease their motion. In gases, the motion is of three types: Translational Motion, Rotational Motion, Vibrational Motion. A mono atomic molecule (e.g. He), will show only translational motion while a diatomic molecule (e.g. O2, N2, etc.) and polyatomic molecules (CO2, NH3 etc.) will undergo, in addition to translational motion, rotational and vibrational motions too. Translational Motion: The motion imparted to the gaseous molecules due to their motion in all possible directions is called translational motion and the energy as kinetic translational energy. In this motion, the molecules move from place to place. Rotational Motion: The motion imparted to the gaseous molecules as a result of net angular momentum about centre of gravity is called rotational motion and the energy as kinetic rotational energy. Vibrational Motion: The to and fro motion of gaseous molecules due to the oscillations is called vibrational motion and the energy as vibrational energy. In this case the molecules vibrate about their mean position. There is continuous interchange between kinetic energy and potential energy. The vibrational motions in polyatomic molecules (like CO2, NH3 etc.) are quite complicated.

ABSOLUTE TEMPERATURE SCALE ON THE BASIS OF CHARLE'S LAW
The Charle's Law states that the volume of a given mass of a gas increases or decreases by 1/273 times of its original volume at 0°C and reduces to zero at -273.15°C. Actually at this temperature a gas does not remain in gaseous state but changes into the liquid or a solid. Lord Kelvin (1824-1907) succeeded in measuring temperature of the gas with the help of a new scale called the Absolute Kelvin temperature scale or the Kelvin temperature scale. According to this scale, -273.15°C is the starting point of the scale. On comparing with the Celsius scale, (which starts with 0°C), the two scales are related as K = °C + 273.

Example: Convert the following Celsius temperatures to Kelvin temperatures: (a) -132°C (b) 96°C (c) 0°C (d) -12°C (e) 148°C. Using the formula: K = °C + 273. (a) K = -132 + 273 = 141K (b) K = 96 + 273 = 369K (c) K = 0 + 273 = 273K (d) K = -12 + 273 = 261K (e) K = 148 + 273 = 421K.

Example: Convert the following Kelvin temperatures to Celsius degree centigrade temperatures: (a) 300K (b) 200K (c) 10K (d) 405K (e) 273K. Using the formula: °C = K - 273. (a) °C = 300 - 273 = 27°C (b) °C = 200 - 273 = -73°C (c) °C = 10 - 273 = -263°C (d) °C = 405 - 273 = 132°C (e) °C = 273 - 273 = 0°C.

Relationship between Temperature and Average Kinetic Energy
The temperature of a substance depends upon kinetic energy of the molecules. The increase in temperature increases the average kinetic energy of the molecules and vice versa. Relationship between Kinetic Energy and Temperature: The kinetic energy of the gaseous molecules is re-distributed with rise or fall of temperature. This can be explained with the help of kinetic equation of gases. Kinetic equation of gases: According to the kinetic molecular theory, the pressure exerted by a gas is given by P = (1/3) (N/V) m c^2. Where P = Pressure, N = Number of molecules, V = Volume of gas, m = mass of one molecule, c = root mean square velocity. This can be written as PV = (1/3) N m c^2. But (1/2) m c^2 = Average kinetic energy per molecule (K.E). So, PV = (2/3) N (K.E). For one mole of gas, it will possess Avogadro's number (Na) of molecules, so N = Na. Therefore PV = (2/3) Na (K.E). According to the General Gas Equation, PV = nRT. For one mole of a gas, n=1 then PV = RT. Comparing equations we get, (2/3) Na (K.E) = RT or K.E = (3/2) (R/Na) T. But R/Na = k, a constant quantity. So, K.E = (3/2) k T. The temperature of a gas is actually the measure of average translational K.E of molecules. The above relation of temperature explains the average translational energy of the molecules. For solids and liquids where molecules cannot move freely, temperature becomes a measure of average vibrational energy.

Gas Pressure
Molecules are in constant random motion. As they move, they collide with one another and the walls of the container. Each collision exerts a force. Over time this must be counterbalanced by a force exerted by the container. This force is called Pressure. The force exerted over a unit area is called Pressure. P = F/A, where P is measured in Pascal, Force in Newton and area in m^2. One Pascal (Pa) is defined as the force of one Newton (N) spread over an area of 1m^2.

Units of Pressure: The SI unit for pressure is Pascal (Pa). 1 atm = 101325 Pa = 101325 Nm^-2. 1 atm = 14.7 Psi (Pounds per square inch). 1 atm = 101.325 kPa. 1 atm = 760 torr = 760 mm of Hg. 1 J = 1 Nm = 10^7 ergs = kg m^2 s^-2. 1 Cal = 4.18 J. 1 atm = 1.01325 bar.

Example: Convert 10 Pascals into (i) Kilo Pascals (ii) atmosphere. Solution: (i) One Kilo Pascal is 1000 Pascals. So 10 Pa = (1/1000) x 10 = 0.01 Kilo Pascal. (ii) One atmosphere = 101325 Pa. So 10 Pa = (1/101325) x 10 = 0.0000987 atm.

Effect of Change in Pressure on the Volume of a Gas (Boyle's Law)
Robert Boyle (1627-1691) established the relationship between the pressure and volume of a gas. According to Boyle's Law, the volume of a given mass of a gas is inversely proportional to the pressure at constant temperature. V ∝ 1/P or PV = k, where k is proportionality constant. This shows that the product of volume and pressure remains constant. For initial and final conditions, P1V1 = P2V2, where V1 and P1 are initial volume and pressure while P2 and V2 are final pressure and volume.

If a graph of pressure of a gas is plotted against volume at different temperatures, we get a curve as shown in the figure. Each curve is a hyperbola with different values of k. The curve is also called isotherm (constant temperature plot). As the temperature is increased, the curve moves away from both the axis. This is because at higher temperature, the volume of a gas increases.

Example: A sample of gas occupies 0.300 dm^3 at a pressure of 1.80 x 10^5 Pa. What is the volume of the gas at the same temperature if the pressure is reduced to 1.15 x 10^5 Pa? Solution: P1 = 1.80 x 10^5 Pa, V1 = 0.300 dm^3, P2 = 1.15 x 10^5 Pa, V2 = ? According to Boyle's law: P1V1 = P2V2. So V2 = (P1V1)/P2 = (1.80 x 10^5 Pa x 0.300 dm^3) / (1.15 x 10^5 Pa) = 0.4696 dm^3.

Example: At 755 torr, volume of a gas is 2m^3. Find its volume if pressure is (a) 100 torr (b) 10 torr. Solution: (a) P1 = 755 torr, V1 = 2 m^3, P2 = 100 torr, V2 = ? According to Boyle's law: P1V1 = P2V2. So V2 = (P1V1)/P2 = (2 m^3 x 755 torr) / 100 torr = 15.10 m^3. (b) P2 = 10 torr. V2 = (2 m^3 x 755 torr) / 10 torr = 151 m^3.

Effect of Change in Temperature on the Volume of a Gas (Charles's Law)
The thermal expansion of gases was studied by Jacques Charles (1746-1823). He derived the relationship between volume of a gas and temperature which is known as Charles's law. It states that at constant pressure, the volume of a given mass of a gas varies linearly with the absolute temperature. Let the volume of an ideal gas at 0°C is Vo cm^3. Then according to Charles's law, at -1°C, its volume would be Vo - (Vo/273). At -273°C, its volume would be Vo - (Vo/273) x 273 = 0. So at exactly -273°C, the volume of a gas reduces to zero. A graph between volume and temperature gives a straight line and cuts the temperature axis at -273°C. This is the hypothetical temperature at which a given volume of a gas reduces to zero. A new temperature scale has been proposed. This new scale is called Absolute or Kelvin scale. It starts from -273.15°C which is taken as zero Kelvin. -273.15°C = Zero Kelvin. This is applicable for all other gases as well and is the lowest possible temperature in the gaseous state but actually it does not happen because all the gases liquify or solidify before they reach this temperature (-273.15°C). This temperature is considered as the lowest temperature.

Charles's Law may also be stated as, "the volume of a definite quantity of a gas at constant pressure is directly proportional to the absolute temperature." V ∝ T or V = kT, where T is the temperature on absolute scale. V/T = k. Charles's Law may also be defined as, "the ratio between volume and temperature is a constant quantity." V1/T1 = V2/T2.

Example: A 50 cm^3 of a gas in a syringe at 15°C is heated to 50°C and the plunger of syringe is allowed to move outwards against constant atmospheric pressure, calculate the new volume of the gas. Solution: V1 = 50 cm^3, T1 = 15°C + 273 = 288K, T2 = 50°C + 273 = 323K, V2 = ? According to Charles's Law: V1/T1 = V2/T2. So V2 = (V1 x T2)/T1 = (50 cm^3 x 323K) / 288K = 56.08 cm^3.

Example: A certain quantity of a gas occupied 125 cm^3 at 0°C. What will be its volume at 100°C if pressure remains constant? Solution: V1 = 125 cm^3, T1 = 0°C + 273 = 273K, T2 = 100°C + 273 = 373K, V2 = ? V2 = (V1 x T2)/T1 = (125 cm^3 x 373K) / 273K = 170.78 cm^3.

AVOGADRO'S LAW
An Italian physicist Amedeo Avogadro (1776-1856) in 1811 proposed that a given volume of a gas at same temperature and pressure must contain the same number of independent particles. These particles may be atoms, molecules or both. He proposed that if we take equal volumes of two gases at a fixed temperature and pressure, the number of moles of both gases will always be the same. It means volume is directly proportional to the number of molecules, where v is volume of the gas and n are number of moles of independent entities. V ∝ n or V = k n.

Avogadro's Law states that equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal number of molecules. The number of particles (atoms, molecules) has been calculated in one mole of a gas and may be called as Avogadro's number and its value is 6.022 x 10^23 particles per mole. It was confirmed by study of diatomic gases. It should be noted that one mole of H2 (2.016g) contains 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. According to Avogadro's law, one mole of any gas at S.T.P. occupies a volume of 22.4 dm^3. For H2, 2.016g occupies 22.4 dm^3 at S.T.P. So 1g of H2 at S.T.P = (1/2.016) x 22.4 dm^3 = 11.2 dm^3. One molecule or one mole of H2 will have 22.4 dm^3 at S.T.P. This volume at S.T.P is called Molar volume. Similarly, one mole of any gas at S.T.P. occupies a volume of 22.4 dm^3. But one mole of H2 will contain 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. Other examples are as following: One mole of N2 (28 g) at S.T.P. = 22.4 dm^3 = 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. One mole of Cl2 (71g) at S.T.P. = 22.4 dm^3 = 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. One mole of O2 (32 g) at S.T.P. = 22.4 dm^3 = 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. One mole of CO2 (44 g) at S.T.P. = 22.4 dm^3 = 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. The standard temperature is 0°C and pressure is one atmosphere.

Example: Calculate the (i) number of molecules and atoms (ii) Number of moles (iii) Volume in dm^3 of 10g of ammonia at S.T.P. Solution: According to Avogadro's Law, one mole of any gas at S.T.P. contains 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. For ammonia one mole contains 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. Mass of NH3 = 10g. Molecular mass of NH3 = 17. Number of moles of NH3 = mass/molecular mass = 10/17 = 0.588 moles. One mole of NH3 at S.T.P. = 6.022 x 10^23 molecules. So 0.588 moles of NH3 at S.T.P. = 0.588 x 6.022 x 10^23 molecules = 3.54 x 10^23 molecules. Number of atoms = no of molecules x atomicity (Number of atoms in one molecule of NH3 is 4) = 3.54 x 10^23 x 4 = 14.16 x 10^23 atoms. One mole of gas at S.T.P = 22.4 dm^3. So 0.588 moles at S.T.P = 0.588 x 22.4 dm^3 = 13.17 dm^3.

IDEAL GAS EQUATION OR EQUATION OF STATE FOR AN IDEAL GAS
An equation that shows the effects of simultaneous changes in pressure and temperature on the volume of given amount of a gas is called ideal gas equation or equation of state for an ideal gas.

Derivation of Ideal Gas Equation
Ideal Gas Equation is a combination of three Laws: 1. Boyle's law, 2. Charles's Law, 3. Avogadro's Law. According to Boyle's Law: V ∝ 1/P (at constant T and n). According to Charles's Law: V ∝ T (at constant P and n). According to Avogadro's Law: V ∝ n (at constant P and T). Combining these three equations, we get V ∝ nT/P or PV ∝ nT. PV = nRT, where R is a constant called general gas constant. This equation is called general gas equation or ideal gas equation. For initial and final conditions: (P1V1)/T1 = (P2V2)/T2.

We cannot compare volumes unless they are measured at same temperature and pressure. Therefore volumes are usually compared at 0°C (273K) and at 1 atm. This condition is called standard temperature and pressure (STP).

Example: A certain mass of a gas occupies 1000 cm^3 at 57°C and 726 mm of Hg. What will be the volume at S.T.P? Solution: P1 = 726 mm of Hg, V1 = 1000 cm^3, T1 = 57°C + 273 = 330K. P2 = 760 mm of Hg, T2 = 273K, V2 = ? Apply the general gas equation: (P1V1)/T1 = (P2V2)/T2. So V2 = (P1V1 T2)/(T1 P2) = (726 mm of Hg x 1000 cm^3 x 273K) / (330K x 760 mm of Hg) = 790.3 cm^3. The volume of the gas at S.T.P = 790.3 cm^3.

General Gas Equation in terms of Density
We know PV = nRT. But n = w/M, where w = mass of the gas, M = molecular mass of the gas. Therefore PV = (w/M) RT or PM = (w/V) RT. But w/V = d (density). So PM = dRT or d = PM/RT. If the values of P, T and M are known, the density can be calculated.

Value of R
R can be calculated in different ways as shown below: If one mole of a gas is taken at S.T.P (273K, 1 atm), then the volume occupied by it is 22.4 dm^3. According to the general gas equation, PV = nRT. So R = PV/nT = (1 atm x 22.4 dm^3) / (1 mole x 273K) = 0.0821 dm^3 atm mole^-1 K^-1. If pressure is measured in mm of Hg or torr and V in cm^3 then R = (760 mm of Hg x 22400 cm^3) / (1 mole x 273K) = 62400 cm^3 mm of Hg mole^-1 K^-1. In SI units, P = 101325 Nm^-2, V = 0.0224 m^3, n = 1 mole, T = 273K. So R = (101325 Nm^-2 x 0.0224 m^3) / (1 mole x 273K) = 8.3143 Nm mole^-1 K^-1. Since 1 Nm = 1 J, so R = 8.3143 J mole^-1 K^-1.

Example: Calculate the average molar mass of air at sea level and 0°C if the density of air is 1.29 kg m^-3. At sea level the pressure may be taken equal to 1 atm or 101325 Pa. We know that d = PM/RT or M = dRT/P. d = 1.29 kg m^-3, R = 8.3143 Nm mole^-1 K^-1, T = 273K, P = 101325 Nm^-2. M = (1.29 kg m^-3 x 8.3143 Nm mole^-1 K^-1 x 273K) / 101325 Nm^-2 = 0.02896 kg mole^-1 = 28.96 g mole^-1.

Example: Calculate the volume of 2 moles of an ideal gas at 25°C and 720 mm pressure. Solution: n = 2 moles, T = 25°C + 273 = 298K, P = 720/760 atm = 0.947 atm, R = 0.0821 dm^3 atm mole^-1 K^-1. Using PV = nRT, V = nRT/P = (2 moles x 0.0821 dm^3 atm mole^-1 K^-1 x 298K) / 0.947 atm = 51.7 dm^3.

Example: 4g of CH4 at 27°C and a pressure of 2.5 atm occupies a volume of 2.46 dm^3. Calculate the value of R. Solution: Mass of CH4 = 4g, Molecular mass of CH4 = 16. Moles of CH4 = mass/molecular mass = 4/16 = 0.25 moles. T = 27°C + 273 = 300K, P = 2.5 atm, V = 2.46 dm^3. Using PV = nRT, R = PV/nT = (2.5 atm x 2.46 dm^3) / (0.25 moles x 300K) = 0.082 dm^3 atm mole^-1 K^-1.

Example: Calculate the density of ammonia at 100°C and 800 mm of Hg pressure. Solution: M of NH3 = 17 g mole^-1, T = 100°C + 273 = 373K, P = 800/760 atm = 1.053 atm, R = 0.0821 dm^3 atm mole^-1 K^-1. Using d = PM/RT = (1.053 atm x 17 g mole^-1) / (0.0821 dm^3 atm mole^-1 K^-1 x 373K) = 0.584 g dm^-3.

DEVIATION FROM IDEAL GAS BEHAVIOUR
A gas which obeys the general gas equation (PV = nRT) and all the gas laws especially Boyle's Law and Charles's Law is called an ideal gas. No such gas is known. In reality no gas is ideal because they show deviations at low temperature and high pressure. The gases which do not obey general gas equation and all the gas laws strictly are called non-ideal gases. All known gases are found to be real under certain conditions.

Why Real gases Deviate from the Gas Laws?
The following table shows the volumes of hydrogen gas at different pressures and at constant temperature. This data does not verify Boyle's law because the product PV is quite different, temperature remains constant. Real gases show deviation from ideal behaviour at low temperature and high pressure. Van der Waals attributed the deviation of real gases from ideal behaviour due to two faulty assumptions in kinetic molecule theory of gases. 1. Intermolecular Forces of attraction: It is assumed that there are no attractive or repulsive forces between the gas molecules. 2. Volume of the gas molecules is negligible as compared to the volume of the container.

At low temperature, the kinetic energy of the molecules decreases. The intermolecular forces of attraction become significant as the molecules come close to each other. At certain temperature, the gases change into the liquid state. Therefore attractive forces between the molecules become significant near liquefying temperature. That is why gases show deviation from their original behaviour at low temperature e.g., SO2 at -10°C while H2 at -252.7°C. Therefore attractive forces between SO2 molecules cannot be considered negligible at room temperature. For this reason SO2 shows more deviation from ideal behaviour at room temperature as compared to H2 gas.

At high pressure, actual volume of gas molecules is very small as compared to the volume of the container. However, this volume does not remain negligible at high pressure. This can be understood from the following figure. At normal pressure is one atm, individual volume of one mole of gas molecules say 32 cm^3 is negligible as compared to the total volume of gas 22400 cm^3. But if it is subjected to a pressure of 100 atm, the volume of the gas is reduced to 224 cm^3. Under this pressure, the volume of gas molecules (32 cm^3) is not negligible as compared to volume of the gas (224 cm^3).

Graphical Representation
For one mole of a gas, if a graph is plotted between PV and pressure, then for an ideal gas, it results in a straight line (the temperature remains constant). But practically, it is seen that the gases do not give a straight line; they deviate from their ideal behaviour as shown in figure. When the pressure is initially increased, the molecules come closer and increases the intermolecular forces. Due to these forces, the volume decreases more than that predicted from the Boyle's Law. This will decrease the product PV. That is why the gases do not show a straight line. The ratio PV/nRT is called compressibility factor. For an ideal gas, it would be one, whereas for real gases it is not equal to one.

VAN DER WAALS EQUATION (MODIFIED FORM OF IDEAL GAS EQUATION)
Since the ideal gas equation is not applicable to real gases, a Dutch Scientist Van der Waals modified it and proposed a new equation called Van der Waals equation. According to him, when a gas is compressed the molecules are brought close to each other. When pressure is further increased, the molecules come in contact with themselves. This is because the molecules have definite volume which is small as compared to the volume of vessel, but it is not negligible. So, he postulated that the actual volume of the molecules per mole of gas is 'b', so the volume available to gas molecules is the volume of vessel minus the volume of molecules. So, Available volume = V - nb, where b is individual volume of one mole of the gas molecules and 'n' is total no. of moles of the gas molecules. It is interesting to note that b is not equal to the actual volume of gas. In fact it is four times the actual volume of molecules (b = 4Vm).

Pressure correction: A molecule in the interior of a gas is attracted by other molecules on all sides, so these attractive forces are cancelled out. However, when a molecule is about to strike the walls of the container, it experiences a force of attraction towards the other molecules of the gas, so the molecules cannot hit the walls of the vessels with that much force with which they should have been hitting in the absence of these attractive forces. It means that the pressure being observed on the walls of the vessels is a little bit less than the ideal pressure. Therefore, P observed = P ideal - P lessened. If the ideal pressure is designated by P, and the pressure lessened due to molecular attractions is denoted by P' then, P observed = P - P' and P = P observed + P'. Van der Waals determined that the forces of attraction between molecules which are striking the walls of container (say type A) and molecules which are pulling them inward (say type B). The force of attraction is proportional to the concentration of type A and type B molecules. P' ∝ C1 . C2. Where C1 and C2 are concentrations of A and B type molecules. But C1 = n/V and C2 = n/V. So P' ∝ (n/V)^2 or P' = a (n^2/V^2), where 'a' is constant of proportionality and is called as co-efficient of attraction.

Van der Waals equation for one mole of a gas is: (P + a/V^2)(V - b) = RT. For 'n' moles of a gas, the equation becomes: (P + n^2a/V^2)(V - nb) = nRT.

Units of 'a' and 'b': Since a = P V^2 / n^2, its units are atm dm^6 mole^-2. Since b is incompressible volume per mole of a gas, its units should be dm^3 mole^-1.

Example: One mole of methane gas is maintained at 300K. Its volume is 250 cm^3. Calculate the pressure of the gas under the following conditions: (a) If the gas is ideal. (b) If the gas is non-ideal, and a = 2.283 dm^6 atm mole^-2, b = 0.0428 dm^3 mole^-1. Solution: (a) If gas is ideal, ideal gas equation is applied. n = 1 mole, V = 0.25 dm^3, T = 300K, R = 0.0821 dm^3 atm mole^-1 K^-1. P = nRT/V = (1 mole x 0.0821 dm^3 atm mole^-1 K^-1 x 300K) / 0.25 dm^3 = 98.52 atm. (b) If the gas is non-ideal (Here Van der Waals equation is applied). (P + n^2a/V^2)(V - nb) = nRT. So P = (nRT/(V - nb)) - (n^2a/V^2). n=1, R=0.0821, T=300K, V=0.25 dm^3, a=0.2283 dm^6 atm mole^-2, b=0.0428 dm^3 mole^-1. P = [(1 x 0.0821 x 300)/(0.25 - 0.0428)] - [(1^2 x 0.2283)/(0.25^2)] = (24.63/0.2072) - (0.2283/0.0625) = 118.87 - 3.6528 = 115.22 atm.

DALTON'S LAW OF PARTIAL PRESSURE AND ITS APPLICATIONS
The English Chemist John Dalton (1766-1844) showed in 1801 that "The total pressure exerted by a gaseous mixture is equal to the sum of partial pressures of each gas." The law is only obeyed, if the component gases do not react chemically. Moreover the gases must behave ideally. The partial pressure is the pressure exerted by a single component of the gaseous mixture. P total = P1 + P2 + P3 + ... Where P1, P2, P3 are partial pressures of individual gases.

Relationship between partial pressure and no. of moles of a gas: Consider a mixture of three gases A, B and C. Let nA, nB and nC be the number of moles of the gases and PA, PB and PC be their partial pressures. According to the general gas equation: PA = nA RT/V, PB = nB RT/V, PC = nC RT/V. P total = (nA + nB + nC) RT/V. Dividing PA by P total: PA/P total = nA/(nA + nB + nC) = mole fraction of A = XA. So PA = XA P total. Partial pressure of any gas = (No. of moles of that gas / Total no. of moles of all gases) x Total Pressure.

Applications:
Collection of gases over water: Dalton's Law is particularly useful when a gas is generated and collected over water provided the gas is insoluble in water. The total pressure consists of the pressure of the water vapours in addition to the pressure of the gas that is generated. The pressure due to water vapour is called aqueous tension. P total = P gas + P water vapour or P gas = P total - P water vapour. The gas contaminated with water may be called a moist gas.

Respiration at higher altitudes: In the respiration process in living things depends upon the differences in partial pressure e.g., partial pressure of O2 in the outside air is higher (159 mmHg) than in the lungs where the partial pressure of O2 is lower (116 mmHg). However at high altitudes, the partial pressure of oxygen decreases and the pilots may have uncomfortable breathing in a non-pressurized cabin where the partial pressure of O2 is about 150 mmHg.

Respiration by deep sea divers: Deep sea divers breathe air under increased pressure. At a depth of 40 metres, the pressure increases 5 times than the normal pressure. Therefore regular air cannot be used in diver's tanks because the partial pressure of O2 would be 795 mm of Hg (159 x 5 = 795 mm of Hg) in that case. Therefore deep sea divers use to breathe a mixture of 96% He and 4% O2 (Heliox) in the respiration tank, the scuba. A scuba (self-contained breathing apparatus having oxygen used by divers underwater) contains compressed air to breath. As diver returns to the surface, it becomes hazardous for him if not handled properly. As the diver comes up, the pressure of the surrounding water drops. Consequently, the compressed air in the lungs expands. Surfacing must be done gently slowly so that the compressed air may escape out from the lungs without causing damage to them. How deep sea divers respire on return from sea: The percentage of N2 in air is about 80%. If N2 is present in diver's tank, the solubility of N2 in blood can increase with increase in pressure in very deep sea. And blood of a diver may be saturated with N2 during a dive. If the diver returns quickly and relatively at low pressure at the surface, he will face life threatening condition called the "bends". Deep sea divers must either use different solutions of gases such as He in O2 or spend many hours in a decompression chamber after a dive. In decompression chamber, the pressure is lowered over many hours. Nitrogen gas then diffuses from the blood and disposed off safely.

Example: A mixture of gases contains 0.5 mole of O2 and 0.3 mole of N2. If the total pressure is 800 mm Hg, find the partial pressure of each gas. Solution: nO2 = 0.5, nN2 = 0.3, n total = 0.8. PO2 = (nO2/n total) x P total = (0.5/0.8) x 800 mm Hg = 500 mm Hg. PN2 = (0.3/0.8) x 800 = 300 mm Hg.

Example: A container has 1 dm^3 of O2 and 2 dm^3 of N2 at 1 atm pressure each at same temperature. Find total pressure if they are transferred to a 1 dm^3 container at same temperature. Solution: For O2: P1 = 1 atm, V1 = 1 dm^3, V2 = 1 dm^3. According to Boyle's law: P1V1 = P2V2, so P2 for O2 = (1 atm x 1 dm^3)/1 dm^3 = 1 atm. For N2: P1 = 1 atm, V1 = 2 dm^3, V2 = 1 dm^3. P2 for N2 = (1 atm x 2 dm^3)/1 dm^3 = 2 atm. Total Pressure = PO2 + PN2 = 1 atm + 2 atm = 3 atm.

Example: 1g of O2 and 2g of N2 are contained in a 5 dm^3 vessel at 27°C. What is the total pressure exerted by the mixture? Solution: Moles of O2 = mass/molecular mass = 1/32 = 0.03125 mole. Moles of N2 = 2/28 = 0.07143 mole. Total moles = 0.03125 + 0.07143 = 0.10268 mole. V = 5 dm^3, T = 27°C + 273 = 300K, R = 0.0821 dm^3 atm K^-1 mole^-1. Using PV = nRT, P = nRT/V = (0.10268 mole x 0.0821 dm^3 atm K^-1 mole^-1 x 300K) / 5 dm^3 = 0.505 atm.

Example: 4.00 mole of N2 and 3.00 moles of H2 are enclosed in a container of volume 10.0 dm^3 at 298K. What are the partial pressures and the total pressure? Solution: nN2 = 4.00 mole, nH2 = 3.00 mole, n total = 7.00 mole, V = 10.0 dm^3, T = 298K, R = 0.0821 dm^3 atm K^-1 mole^-1. P total = n total RT/V = (7.00 mole x 0.0821 dm^3 atm K^-1 mole^-1 x 298K) / 10.0 dm^3 = 17.13 atm. PN2 = (nN2/n total) x P total = (4.00/7.00) x 17.13 atm = 9.79 atm. PH2 = (3.00/7.00) x 17.13 atm = 7.34 atm.

Example: 35 cm^3 of a gas was collected over water at 6°C and 765 mm Hg pressure. Calculate its volume at S.T.P. (V.P. of water at 6°C = 7 mm of Hg). Solution: P1 = 765 - 7 = 758 mm Hg, V1 = 35 cm^3, T1 = 6°C + 273 = 279K. P2 = 760 mm Hg, T2 = 273K, V2 = ? Using (P1V1)/T1 = (P2V2)/T2, V2 = (P1V1 T2)/(T1 P2) = (758 mm Hg x 35 cm^3 x 273K) / (279K x 760 mm Hg) = 34.1 cm^3.

DIFFUSION AND EFFUSION
The spontaneous intermixing of molecules of one gas with another at a given temperature and pressure is called Diffusion. Diffusion is the random movement of a gas from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration. Examples: Fragrance of rose. Intermixing of NH3 and HCl gases gives dense white fumes of NH4Cl. Reddish brown bromine Br2 gas taken in a test tube will combine with air taken in another test tube. Red vapours of Br2 gas will spread in the test tube containing air. When a drop of liquid ink is left into bottom of test tube containing water, it will spread evenly throughout the water.

Effusion is the escape of gaseous molecules through extremely small pores in a region of low pressure. This escaping of molecules is not due to collisions but due to their motion one by one.

GRAHAM'S LAW OF DIFFUSION AND EFFUSION
A Scottish scientist Thomas Graham in 1831 (1805-1869), measured the rates at which different gases effuse from an apparatus at the same temperature and pressure. He found that the lighter the gas (the lighter its particles), the faster the gas escaped from the apparatus.

Mathematical form of Graham's Law of diffusion of gases: The rate of diffusion or effusion of a gas is inversely proportional to the square root of its density at constant temperature and pressure. r ∝ 1/√d, where r = rate of diffusion, d = density of the gas. But density is directly proportional to molar mass M. So r ∝ 1/√M or r = k/√M, where k is constant of proportionality.

Consider two gases with r1 and r2 as their rates of diffusion and d1, d2 be their densities and molar masses M1, M2 respectively. For two different gases, equation can be written as r1 = k/√M1 and r2 = k/√M2. Dividing equation, r1/r2 = √(M2/M1). It may also be written as r1/r2 = √(d2/d1).

Example: Equal volumes of HCl and SO2 are confined in a porous container. What would be the comparative rates of diffusion of these gases through the porous walls? The molecular masses of HCl and SO2 are 36.5 and 64 respectively. Solution: Molecular mass of HCl (M1) = 36.5 amu. Molecular mass of SO2 (M2) = 64 amu. Rate of diffusion of HCl (r1) = ? Rate of diffusion of SO2 (r2) = ? According to the Graham's Law of Diffusion: r1/r2 = √(M2/M1) = √(64/36.5) = √1.753 = 1.324. So r1 = 1.324 r2. The rate of HCl will be 1.324 times the rate of diffusion of SO2.

LIQUEFACTION OF GASES
Gases can be liquefied by Joule-Thomson's effect. The liquefaction of gases can be achieved by cooling them to low temperature. When a highly compressed gas is allowed to escape through a throttle (small hole), the temperature falls to such an extent that it liquefies. At low temperature, the gaseous molecules come close to each other with the result that intermolecular attractions increase. When it is allowed to escape through a nozzle (small hole) from high pressure to low pressure, the molecules move apart. In doing so, energy is needed to overcome the intermolecular attractions. This energy is taken from the molecules themselves, so the gas is cooled. This process is repeated for many times until the gas completely changes into the liquid form.

Linde's Method (1895)
According to Joule-Thomson's effect "When a highly compressed gas is allowed to escape out through a throttle, the temperature of the gas falls to such an extent, that it changes into the liquid form". This is the basic principle of adiabatic expansion which is thermally isolated from its surroundings.

Steps: Air is compressed to 200 atm approx. Most of the water in the air condenses and is removed. Then the heat generated as a result of compression is removed by passing the gas through cooling pipes. The dry gas is then passed through a copper spiral coil. It is then expanded to almost atmospheric pressure through a controlled valve. When the air comes out the valve the expansion takes place from 200 atm to 1 atm. In this, a fall of temperature occurs. This cold air goes up and cools the incoming compressed air. This cycle is repeated several times. Ultimately the expanding gas finally drops and the remaining air is liquefied. The condensed air collects in the chamber and can be drawn off.

Claude's Method
The principle of appliances such as refrigerators, heat pumps and air conditioners all work on the principle of Joule-Thomson's effect. A compressor compresses a gas such as Freon. The hot Freon gas passes through coils on the outside of the refrigerator. Air at room temperature cools down the hot Freon and as a result condenses it. The liquid Freon then passes through an expansion valve into low pressure tubes which are inside the refrigerator. As the liquid Freon evaporates rapidly, the heat of vaporisation needed to do this is drawn from the refrigerator and its contents, thereby cooling. Then the Freon gas is once again drawn into the compressor and the cycle starts over again.

Example: Cooling pop can (container). A small container holding liquid CO2 is built right into the can. When the can is opened, the liquid CO2 vaporises and escapes out of the top of the can. The heat absorbed by the vaporising CO2 can lower the temperature of the POP by about 16°C in a few seconds. Thus the temperature is lowered considerably.

Applications of Kinetic Molecular Theory
The Velocity of molecules: The equation for the root mean square velocity deduced from kinetic equation is: Crms = √(3RT/M). Where Crms = root mean square velocity, M = molecular mass of the gas, T = absolute temperature. This equation gives a quantitative relationship between the absolute temperature and the velocities of gas molecules. Higher the temperature of a gas, greater would be the velocities of the molecules.

Graham's Law of diffusion of gases: According to the postulates of Kinetic Molecular Theory, the Kinetic Energy of the particles is proportional to the Absolute Temperature. (1/2) m v^2 = (3/2) k T. So v = √(3kT/m). At constant temperature, K.E will remain constant so √(3kT) = Constant. Therefore v ∝ 1/√m. Thus the kinetic molecular theory predicts that the average speed depends upon molecular mass (m). Further the average speed of the particles is inversely proportional to the square root of their molecular mass.

PLASMA (THE FOURTH STATE OF MATTER)
Plasma is the fourth state of matter. It was identified by William Crooks in 1879. It is molecular gas changing into atomic form and then to ionic form at high temperature. Molecular gas → Atomic gas → Ions. At a temperature of 10000K to 100000K (10eV to 100eV) electrons are removed from atoms. Plasma is composed of a mixture of un-ionized gas, free electrons and charged particles. Most of the universe contains matter in the Plasma state e.g., shining stars and interstellar space is filled with plasma. On our planet plasma does not exist. Plasma is fourth state of matter.

Properties of plasma: It is a substance in which many of the atoms or molecules are ionised effectively making it easy for electrical charges to flow freely. It takes place at very high temperature. Plasma has the following properties: Plasma consists of neutral particles, positive ions and free electrons. Plasma is strongly influenced by both magnetic and electric force. Plasma shows a characteristic glow depending upon the gas present in the discharge tube e.g., Oxygen gives a red glow, hydrogen green and Nitrogen purple or pink glow.
