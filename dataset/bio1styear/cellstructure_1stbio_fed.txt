
CELL STRUCTURE AND FUNCTIONS

All organisms are familiar with the word “cell”, i.e., a basic unit of life. By the middle of the nineteenth century, biologists had formulated cell theory which is a fundamental concept of biology. The generally accepted portions of the modern cell theory are as follows:

(i) The cell is the fundamental unit of structure and function in living things.
(ii) All organisms are made up of one or more cells.
(iii) Cells arise from other cells through cellular division.

This chapter will help you to become familiar with the structure of cells and how they work, and also the basic techniques essential for cell study.

1.1 TECHNIQUES USED IN CELL BIOLOGY

To know the structure and functions of cells and cell organelles, some of the techniques will be discussed here in brief.

1.1.1 Cell Fractionation

Cell fractionation is the combination of various methods used to separate cell organelles and components based upon size and density. It is very useful for electron microscopy of cell components. The principle of cell fractionation consists of two steps, i.e., homogenization and centrifugation.

Homogenization

It is the formation of a homogeneous mass of cells. It involves the grinding of cells in a suitable medium with correct pH, ionic composition and temperature. In plants, enzyme pectinase is added to digest middle lamella. This can be done in a blender. This procedure gives rise to a uniform mixture of cells which is then centrifuged.

Centrifugation

Centrifugation is the process to separate substances on the basis of their size and densities under the influence of centrifugal force. It is done by the machine called centrifuge. This machine can spin the tubes at very high speed. Spinning the tubes exerts a centrifugal force on the contents. There are two major ways of centrifugation, i.e., density gradient centrifugation and differential centrifugation.

In density gradient centrifugation, the cell components of different sizes and densities are separated in different layers. The upper layers are less dense than the lower layers.

In differential centrifugation, the size and shape of particles determine how fast it settles. A series of increasing speeds can be used. At each step, the contents which settle at the bottom of the tube are called pellet and those that remain suspended above in the form of liquid are called supernatant. After each speed, the supernatant can be drawn off and centrifuged again. A series of pellets containing cell organelles of smaller and smaller size can therefore be obtained.

Bigger particles sediment faster and have higher sedimentation coefficients (Svedberg units). Sedimentation coefficients are, however, not additive. Sedimentation rate depends on the shape and volume of a particle, and when two particles bind together there is an increase in sedimentation. Thus, when measured separately they will have Svedberg values that may not add up in the compound particle. This is notably the case with the ribosome. Ribosomes are most often identified by their sedimentation coefficient. For instance, the 70S ribosome that comes from bacteria has an actual sedimentation coefficient of 70 Svedberg, although it is composed of a 50S subunit and a 30S subunit.

1.1.2 Differential Staining

Most biological structures are transparent. In order to differentiate between these structures, various coloured dyes are applied. Such techniques are called staining techniques. When only one stain, such as borax carmine (that stains nucleus), is used, it is called single staining. When two stains, one that will stain nucleus (e.g., haematoxylin) and other that will stain cytoplasm (e.g., eosin), are used, the process is called double staining or differential staining.

1.1.3 Microdissections

Microdissection refers to the variety of techniques where a microscope is used to assist in dissection. It is generally used to remove tumour or granules from delicate tissues or cells like brain, heart and nerve cells. In this technique, the image is seen on a large TV screen or monitor while dissecting.

1.1.4 Tissue Culture

Growth of a cell or a tissue on a chemical medium under controlled conditions is called tissue culture. This technique can be employed for plants and animals.

Plant tissue culturing is mainly used for plant cloning, i.e., production of identical plants (clones). Animal tissue culture is usually set up by growing individual cells over the surface of a glass container. Animal tissue cultures are used to sense any abnormality in the cell, e.g., cancer, chromosomal disorder, etc.

1.1.5 Chromatography

Chromatography is a technique which is used to separate different chemical compounds from a mixture. It is generally used for the separation of mixtures of proteins, amino acids or photosynthetic pigments. There are different types of chromatographic techniques. Paper chromatography is a simple and most widely used technique. It involves two phases: stationary phase, which is cellulose filter paper, and mobile phase, which is solvent in which sample mixture is dissolved. When the solvent travels over the paper, the mixture sample begins to separate and appears as dots at different places on paper according to their affinity. This paper is then called chromatogram.

1.1.6 Electrophoresis

Electrophoresis is a technique which is used to separate charged polymer molecules on the basis of their size, shape, molecular weight and surface charge whether positive (+) or negative (–). Such charged polymer molecules include DNA, RNA and proteins.

This technique utilizes a gel medium for the separation of fragments which is done under the influence of an electric field. Often the gel is sandwiched between glass or plastic plates to form a viscous slab. The two ends of the slab are suspended in two salt solutions that are connected by electrodes to a power source. At one end of the slab the samples are loaded. When voltage is applied to the apparatus, the molecules present in the gel migrate through the electric field.

The negatively charged molecules move towards the positive pole and the positively charged molecules move towards the negative pole. The velocity of movement of fragments is inversely proportional to the size. Therefore, smaller fragments move faster than larger ones. In this way all the fragments are separated in the gel after some time. Later on the molecules can be pinpointed by staining the gel.

1.1.7 Spectrophotometry

Spectrophotometry is a technique which is used to determine the absorption of different wavelengths of light by a particular chemical compound or a photosynthetic pigment. For this purpose, the instrument used is called spectrophotometer. The amount of light absorbed at each wavelength is plotted in a graph called the absorption spectrum.

Spectrophotometry can be used to determine the wavelengths of light that take part in photosynthesis. It can also be used to determine the very minute quantity of a substance.


1.1.8 Resolution and Magnification in Microscopy

The minimum capacity of a lens to differentiate between two adjacent points is called resolution. The resolution of the naked eye is 0.1 mm. This resolution can be increased by increasing magnification.

Magnification is the capacity of an optical instrument to increase the size of an object more than its original size. The objects which cannot be seen by the naked eye can also be observed by increasing magnification. Different lenses have different magnification powers which are represented by the letter “X”, meaning the number of times larger than the original size. Therefore, a lens of 10X magnification power can increase the size of an object from 4 µm to 40 µm.

Electron microscopy is the technique used to visualize objects that cannot be seen by the naked eye or light microscope. A common compound microscope consists of ocular and objective lenses. The magnification power of such a microscope is equal to the product of magnification powers of both lenses.


The resolving power of a light microscope is 250 nm and its magnification is up to 4000X. The resolving power of an electron microscope is 0.2 nm and its magnification is up to 2,000,000X.

1.1.9 Micrometry

Micrometry is the measurement of the size of objects under a microscope. It involves the use of two micrometers. The ocular micrometer is a glass disc with 100 equal divisions with no absolute value. It is placed in the eyepiece of the microscope. Then it is calibrated by using a stage micrometer.

The stage micrometer is a glass slide with an exact scale like a miniature transparent ruler. By superimposing the images of the ocular micrometer and stage micrometer scales, it is calibrated so that the size of any given object viewed under the microscope can be estimated.

1.2 CELL WALL AND PLASMA MEMBRANE

The plasma membrane is the outer living boundary of the cell. Many cells have an extracellular component that is formed exterior to the membrane, which is called the cell wall.

1.2.1 Cell Wall

The cell wall is present in plant cells, prokaryotes and fungi, but animal cells do not have a cell wall. This is probably due to their locomotor mode of life. Plant cell walls made up of cellulose differ in chemical composition from those of prokaryotes made up of peptidoglycan and fungi made up of chitin.

The cell wall is secreted by the cell. It is porous and allows free passage of water and dissolved materials. The plant cell wall consists of three main layers: primary cell wall, middle lamella and secondary cell wall.


Primary Cell Wall

The primary cell wall is a true wall and develops in newly growing cells, i.e., during cell growth. It is present inner to the middle lamella. The primary cell wall is thin and slightly flexible. The primary cell wall is mainly composed of cellulose microfibrils embedded in a matrix of polysaccharides like hemicelluloses and pectin. The microfibrils are arranged in layers one above the other. This feature allows the wall to stretch plastically and reversibly. Therefore, the primary cell wall is adapted to growth.

Secondary Cell Wall

The secondary cell wall is formed between the primary cell wall and plasma membrane only in sclerenchyma cells. The plant cells possessing secondary cell wall are generally dead and provide mechanical support to the plant. The secondary cell wall is formed when the cell has reached its maximum growth because it is very rigid and does not allow further growth.

The secondary cell wall contains lignin, inorganic salts and waxes. Lignin cements and anchors cellulose microfibrils together and provides rigidity to the cell. The secondary cell wall gives strength to the cell and provides definite shape.

Middle Lamella

Middle lamella is a sticky, gel-like layer present between adjacent cells which holds them together. It is composed mainly of pectic acids, calcium and magnesium salts.

1.2.2 Plasma Membrane

The plasma membrane is also called plasmalemma. It is present in all living cells including prokaryotes. It controls the movement of materials into and out of the cell.

1.2.3 Fluid Mosaic Model of Plasma Membrane

The fluid mosaic model of plasma membrane proposes that the membrane consists of a phospholipid bilayer in which protein molecules are either partially or wholly embedded. The phospholipids form a bilayer in which hydrophobic ends face each other while hydrophilic ends face outward.

Proteins are scattered throughout the membrane in an irregular pattern just like large icebergs floating in the sea. The pattern of distribution of proteins can vary from membrane to membrane and also vary on both surfaces of the membrane. The membrane is about 7 nm thick.

The lipid part of plasma membrane consists of two layers of phospholipids. Steroids such as cholesterol are wedged into the phospholipid bilayer at some intervals. The plasma membrane is asymmetrical, i.e., its two surfaces are not identical.

In general, most membrane proteins drift sideways in the fluid bilayer. The proteins within the membrane determine most of the membrane functions. Carbohydrates are attached either to proteins (glycoproteins) or lipids (glycolipids) and are generally present on the outer surface of the membrane. Filaments of the cytoskeleton are also present on the inner surface of the membrane which support the plasma membrane.

Functions of Plasma Membrane Lipids

The lipid part of the plasma membrane controls the fluidity of the membrane. When the concentration of unsaturated fatty acids in phospholipids increases, the bilayer becomes more fluid and the membrane becomes more flexible. Cholesterol helps to stabilize the lipid bilayer and restricts the entry and exit of polar molecules and ions.

Functions of Plasma Membrane Proteins

A variety of proteins are found in plasma membrane which may act as transport proteins, enzymes, receptors or antigens.


Carrier Proteins

Some plasma membrane proteins act as carrier proteins. Some of these have a channel through the membrane, while others bind the substance and help it to move across the membrane.

Enzymes

Some plasma membrane proteins have enzymatic functions, e.g., adenylate cyclase which converts ATP to cyclic AMP (cAMP).

Receptor Molecules

Some proteins in the plasma membrane act as receptors and receive signals from other cells. Each type of receptor has a specific shape. Binding of a molecule to a receptor can bring about an intracellular response. For example, hormones circulate in the blood but bind to specific target cells with specific receptors.

Antigens

Some proteins are antigens which enable the cells to recognize other cells. Foreign antigens can be recognized and attacked by the immune system.

Role of Glycolipids and Glycoproteins as Cell Surface Markers

Mostly glycolipids and glycoproteins act as cell surface markers. They are involved in cell-to-cell recognition and sticking the correct cells together in tissues.

Regulation of Cell’s Interaction with its Environment

The plasma membrane regulates cell’s interaction with its environment by controlling transport of materials across the cell. Transport across plasma membrane occurs to obtain nutrients, excrete waste substances, secrete useful substances, generate ionic gradients essential for nervous and muscular activity, and maintain a suitable pH and ionic concentration within the cell for enzyme activity.


1.3 CYTOPLASM AND CELL ORGANELLES

The living matter of a cell is called protoplasm. In eukaryotic cells it can be divided into two parts, i.e., cytoplasm and nucleus.

Cytoplasm

Cytoplasm is the region between nuclear membrane and plasma membrane. It is present in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells. The major difference between these two kinds of cells is the presence or absence of cytoskeleton and membrane-bound organelles. These structures are absent in prokaryotic cells.

Chemical Nature of Cytoplasm

Cytoplasm contains water and forms a solution that contains all the fundamental biomolecules. Some of these are ions and small molecules such as sugars, amino acids, fatty acids, nucleotides and vitamins which remain in true solution. Others like proteins form colloidal solutions.

The inner portion of cytoplasm is called cytosol while the peripheral region is more viscous and is called ectoplasm.

Cyclosis

The movement of cytoplasm is called cyclosis which is responsible for the distribution of cell organelles.

Metabolic and Storage Role of Cytoplasm

The cytoplasm acts as a site of metabolism and storehouse of the cell. Metabolic reactions generally occur in the cytosol which include protein synthesis and glycolysis. The cytoplasm is also concerned with storage of useful compounds which are subsequently used in cellular activities and waste compounds which are eliminated from the cell from time to time.

1.4 Cell Organelles

In a eukaryotic cell, the cytoplasm contains highly organized discrete structures which are specific for various cellular functions and are called cell organelles. The cell organelles are generally enclosed by membranes except a few such as ribosomes.

The organelles present in the cytoplasmic matrix of a cell are endoplasmic reticulum, ribosomes, Golgi complex, lysosomes, peroxysomes, glyoxysomes, vacuoles, mitochondria and chloroplasts.

Endoplasmic Reticulum

Endoplasmic reticulum is an interconnecting network of tubules and cisternae which is generally extended from nuclear membrane to the plasma membrane throughout the cytoplasm of all eukaryotic cells.

There are two types of endoplasmic reticulum, rough ER and smooth ER. Most cells contain both types of ER. However, some cells like skeletal muscle cells have more smooth ER, where it is called sarcoplasmic reticulum.

Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum

Rough ER has ribosomes attached on the sides facing the cytoplasm and has a rough appearance under electron microscope. Rough ER is mainly concerned with protein synthesis due to the association of ribosomes. It also provides mechanical support to the cell.

Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum

Smooth ER is continuous with rough ER. Since ribosomes are not attached to it, it has a smooth appearance under electron microscope. Smooth ER functions in metabolic processes such as metabolism of carbohydrates, detoxification of drugs especially in liver cells, and synthesis of lipids including oils, phospholipids and steroids.

Smooth ER also stores calcium ions which are released during muscle contraction. It also transports various cellular products within the cell.

Golgi Complex

The Golgi complex is found in all eukaryotic cells. It was discovered by Italian biologist Camillo Golgi. The Golgi complex consists of a stack of flattened, membrane-bound sacs called cisternae, together with a system of associated vesicles known as Golgi vesicles.

The cis face of Golgi complex is convex, while the trans face is concave and is called the maturing face, where the cisternae break up into vesicles.

The most important function of the Golgi complex is the processing of cell secretions. These organelles are abundant in secretory cells. The cell secretions mainly consist of proteins. The Golgi complex collects proteins from rough ER through smooth ER, modifies them to perform specific functions, and then exports these modified products in the form of vesicles.

Certain organelles such as lysosomes, peroxisomes and glyoxysomes also originate from the Golgi complex. The Golgi complex is also involved in the formation of conjugated molecules like glycoproteins and lipoproteins.

In plant cells during cell division, the Golgi complex gives rise to vesicles which contain cell wall synthesizing materials. At cytokinesis, these Golgi vesicles are arranged on the cell equator, fuse together and form a structure called phragmoplast. Later on, a new cell wall is derived from this structure.

Lysosomes

The word lyso means splitting and soma means body. Lysosomes are single membranous, spherical vesicles. They contain digestive or hydrolytic enzymes.

The lysosomal enzymes are synthesized by the rough ER and then transported to the Golgi complex through smooth ER. After modification, these enzymes are released from the trans face of the Golgi complex in the form of vesicles. Such vesicles are called lysosomes.

The newly formed lysosomes before the start of digestion are usually called primary lysosomes. In plants and protists, vacuoles carry out digestion, a function shared by lysosomes in animal cells.

Lysosomes contain about 40 different digestive enzymes. These enzymes digest major macromolecules of the cell. The contents of lysosomes are acidic.

During digestion, lysosomes fuse with membrane-bound vesicles formed by endocytosis, phagocytosis or autophagocytosis. These vesicles are referred to as endosomes, phagosomes and autophagosomes. After fusion with lysosomes, secondary lysosomes are formed. The biomolecules are broken down into smaller components like amino acids, monosaccharides, nucleotides and fatty acids which are then recycled in the cell.

Functions of lysosomes include intracellular digestion, autophagy, autolysis and secretion of extracellular enzymes.

The ingested food of the cell is stored in vesicles called food vacuoles. When a lysosome fuses with a food vacuole, the resulting structure is called secondary lysosome in which digestion begins. The digested products are absorbed by the cytoplasm while the remaining waste-containing vesicle is called residual body. Later on, residual bodies fuse with the cell membrane by exocytosis to eliminate undigested wastes. This process is known as intracellular digestion.

Autophagy

The process by which unwanted structures within the cell are engulfed and digested by lysosomes is called autophagy. It is a self-eating process of the cell in which lysosomes digest the cell’s own organelles. Such lysosomes are also called autophagosomes.

Autophagy may occur during starvation to obtain energy or routinely to control the number of specific organelles. For example, when a person performs heavy muscular exercise, the number of mitochondria increases in muscle cells, but when exercise stops, excess mitochondria are removed by autophagy.

Autolysis

Autolysis occurs during developmental phases when a particular cell type is required to die. This type of cell death is programmed and is called autolysis. Due to this function, lysosomes are also called suicidal bags.


Lysosomal Storage Diseases

If a particular lysosomal enzyme is absent or inactive, digestion of the corresponding substance is affected. As a result, the substance accumulates in the cell and causes serious problems. Such complications caused by the accumulation of substances due to the absence of certain lysosomal enzymes are called lysosomal storage diseases.

These diseases are hereditary and congenital and therefore run in families. Most of these diseases are fatal in early childhood. More than 20 such diseases have been discovered so far. One common example is Tay-Sachs disease, in which a lipid-digesting enzyme is missing or inactive, resulting in accumulation of lipids in brain cells.

Peroxisomes and Glyoxysomes

Peroxisomes and glyoxysomes are collectively called microbodies. These are single membranous vesicular structures similar to lysosomes but smaller in size. They contain enzymes different from lysosomal enzymes and originate from the Golgi complex.

Peroxisomes contain oxidative enzymes such as peroxidases, catalases and glycolic acid oxidase. They are abundant in liver cells and are involved in formation and decomposition of hydrogen peroxide. They are mainly concerned with detoxification of alcohol.

In this process, alcohol is oxidized into hydrogen peroxide with the help of peroxidase enzyme. Hydrogen peroxide is toxic and is immediately broken down into water and oxygen by catalase enzyme.

In plant cells, peroxisomes are involved in photorespiration. A step of photorespiration takes place in peroxisomes in which glycolate is converted into glycine.

Glyoxysomes are found only at the seedling stage in oil seed plants. These organelles contain enzymes specific for plant lipid metabolism. Germinating seedlings convert stored fatty acids into carbohydrates through a metabolic pathway called glyoxylate cycle, the enzymes of which are present in glyoxysomes.


Vacuoles

Vacuoles are large vesicles originating from the endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi complex and plasma membrane. Vacuoles perform a variety of functions in different kinds of cells.

In animal cells, food vacuoles are formed by phagocytosis. Many freshwater protists possess contractile vacuoles that pump excess water out of the cell, thereby maintaining suitable concentration of ions and molecules inside the cell.

In plant cells, vacuoles may also help in protection against herbivores by storing poisonous or unpleasant substances. Mature plant cells generally contain a single large central vacuole formed by the fusion of smaller vacuoles.

The solution inside the vacuole, called cell sap, is the main reservoir of inorganic ions including chloride. The central vacuole plays a major role in mechanical support by maintaining turgor pressure and also acts as a storehouse of the cell. The membrane separating vacuole from cytoplasm is called tonoplast.

Mitochondria

Mitochondria (singular: mitochondrion) are present in all eukaryotic cells. Some cells possess a single large mitochondrion, but more often a cell has hundreds or thousands of mitochondria. The number of mitochondria correlates with the metabolic activity of the cell. Cells that move or contract have more mitochondria.

Mitochondria are self-replicating and divide by fission.

They are cylindrical or rod-shaped structures enclosed by a double membrane: outer membrane and inner membrane. The outer membrane is smooth and freely permeable due to the presence of porins. The inner membrane is selectively permeable and folded inward to form cristae, which increase the surface area.


The inner membrane divides the mitochondrion into two compartments: intermembrane space and mitochondrial matrix. The matrix contains enzymes, small circular DNA, RNA and ribosomes. These components indicate that mitochondria possess their own genetic system.

Mitochondria are the sites of cellular respiration, the metabolic process that extracts energy from sugars, fats and other organic compounds. Enzymes of Krebs cycle are present in the mitochondrial matrix, while enzymes involved in electron transport chain and ATP synthesis are located on the inner mitochondrial membrane.

Endosymbiotic Theory

Mitochondria and chloroplasts show similarities with bacteria. Both are self-replicating, possess their own DNA and ribosomes (70S), and can survive outside the cell under laboratory conditions. These observations support the theory that mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living prokaryotic organisms that were engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells and formed a symbiotic relationship.

Mitochondria are inherited maternally because they are present in the ovum but not in the sperm head.

Plastids

Plastids are found in plant and algal cells. They are essential for processes like photosynthesis and food storage. Based on pigment presence and stage of development, plastids are classified into proplastids, leucoplasts, chromoplasts and chloroplasts.

Proplastids are immature plastids found in meristematic cells. They divide and differentiate into other types of plastids depending on conditions.

Leucoplasts are colourless plastids found in roots, stems and seeds. They store food and are classified into amyloplasts, elaioplasts and proteinoplasts.

Chromoplasts synthesize coloured pigments and are found in flowers and fruits where they help in pollination and seed dispersal.

Chloroplasts are green plastids found in green parts of plants and are the sites of photosynthesis.

Structure and Functions of Chloroplast

Chloroplast is a discoid structure consisting of three parts: envelope, stroma and thylakoids. Each chloroplast is bounded by a double membrane called envelope. The outer membrane is freely permeable while the inner membrane is selectively permeable. Between them lies intermembrane space.

Stroma is the colourless protein-rich matrix of chloroplast. It contains DNA, RNA, ribosomes and enzymes. Thylakoids are membrane-bound flattened sacs present in the stroma.

Thylakoids are of two types: smaller thylakoids and larger thylakoids. Smaller thylakoids are stacked together to form grana. Each granum consists of 25–50 thylakoids. Larger thylakoids connect grana and are called stroma lamellae.

Photosynthetic pigments are present in the membranes of thylakoids. Chloroplast is the site of photosynthesis. Light-dependent reactions occur in the grana, while light-independent reactions occur in the stroma.

Centrioles

Centrioles are non-membranous organelles found mainly in animal cells. They are also present in some fungi and protists. Centrioles are cylindrical structures usually present in pairs at right angles near the nucleus.

Each centriole is composed of nine triplets of microtubules arranged around a central axis. Before cell division, centrioles duplicate and migrate to opposite poles to form spindle fibres which help in chromosome separation.

Centrioles also give rise to basal bodies of cilia and flagella.



Cytoskeleton

Cytoskeleton consists of three types of fibrous structures: microfilaments, microtubules and intermediate filaments.

Microfilaments are thin contractile fibres made of actin protein. They are involved in muscle contraction and relaxation.

Microtubules are hollow cylindrical structures composed of tubulin protein. They play a role in spindle formation, cilia, flagella and centrioles.

Intermediate filaments are thicker than microfilaments but thinner than microtubules. They provide mechanical support to the cell and maintain cell shape.

Cilia and Flagella

Cilia and flagella are hair-like projections on the surface of cells. They have the same internal structure but differ in length and number. Flagella are longer and fewer, while cilia are shorter and numerous.

Both have a common ultrastructure consisting of a central pair of microtubules surrounded by nine pairs, forming a 9+2 arrangement.


Cilia and flagella originate from basal bodies which have the same structure as centrioles.

Movement of Cilia

Movement of cilia occurs due to sliding of microtubule doublets. During effective stroke, cilium bends and moves fluid, while during recovery stroke it returns to original position.

Movement of Flagella

Flagellar movement occurs by propagation of waves from base to tip. This movement helps in locomotion, for example in human sperm cells.

1.3.3 Nucleus

Nucleus is the largest and most important organelle of the cell. In animal cells it is centrally located, while in plant cells it is pushed to the side due to the presence of a large central vacuole.

The eukaryotic nucleus consists of nuclear envelope, nucleoplasm, nucleolus and chromatin.


Nuclear Envelope

Nuclear envelope is a double membrane covering the nucleus. The two membranes are separated by perinuclear space. The outer membrane is continuous with endoplasmic reticulum and contains ribosomes.

Nuclear pores are present in the nuclear envelope and are composed of transport proteins called nucleoporins. These pores regulate the exchange of materials between nucleus and cytoplasm.

Nucleoplasm

Nucleoplasm is the transparent fluid inside the nucleus containing proteins, enzymes, nucleotides and metal ions. It differs slightly from cytoplasm in composition.

Nucleolus

Nucleolus is a non-membranous structure present in nucleoplasm. It is involved in ribosome synthesis and contains rRNA and ribosomal subunits.

Chromatin and Chromosomes

Chromatin is a network of thin thread-like structures made of DNA and proteins. During cell division, chromatin condenses to form chromosomes. Each chromosome consists of two chromatids joined at centromere.


The centromere contains kinetochore proteins which attach spindle fibres during cell division. Some chromosomes also possess secondary constrictions known as nucleolar organizers.

1.4 PROKARYOTIC AND EUKARYOTIC CELLS

Two structurally different types of cells have evolved over time: prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells. Prokaryotic cells include archaea, bacteria and cyanobacteria, while all other forms of life are composed of eukaryotic cells.

Prokaryotic cells lack membrane-bound nucleus and organelles. Their DNA is dispersed in cytoplasm. Eukaryotic cells contain nucleus, mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi complex, lysosomes, cytoskeleton and 80S ribosomes.

Prokaryotic cells divide by binary fission, while eukaryotic cells divide by mitosis and meiosis.
